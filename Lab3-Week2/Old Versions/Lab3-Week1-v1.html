Part I: Hooke's Law

Simple Harmonic Motion (introduction)?

Most springs obey Hooke's Law: the magnitude of the force the spring exerts is proportional to the amount it is stretched. The ratio of the force (FFF) to the amount stretched (ddd) is the spring constant kkk; k=Fdk = \frac{F}{d}k=dF​. This principle applies to more than just springs, it also works when dealing with elastics such as rubber bands.

One way to determine k for a spring or rubber band is to hang a mass m from it. The force that mass is exerting on the rubber band is then mg, where g is the acceleration due to gravity - assume g=9.80ms2g = 9.80 \frac{m}{s^2}g=9.80s2m​. We can then see how far the rubber band stretches.

More quantitatively:

If the graph of |ddd| vs mmm (with m as the independent variable) approximates a straight line, then this is evidence that the rubber band obey's Hooke's Law. Furthermore, the slope of the line, sss, will be gk\frac{g}{k}kg​. You could determine kkk if you found sss: k=gsk = \frac{g}{s}k=sg​.
